So what are my thoughts on how much power to give AI? Questions of human-in-the-loop, how do we trust the agents, how to deal with agentic misalignment. 

Where is the line of what we have AI do, and what do we not let it do? I think a good place to start is to not let AI fully make any irreversible decisions. I think this comes down to the fact that AI doesn’t think predictably. It may simulate human reasoning, but it’s still just a simulation. It can do some crazy things sometimes. It’s dangerous to give an agent power to do something you can’t reverse. I think that one of the issues is that I don’t think AI can actually feel emotions. At least right now, all it can do is simulate thought. So when it encounters an ethical or moral dilemma, it can only think through it logically. It sort of gets trapped into maybe like a utilitarianism mindset, and if it thinks that one path will result out better, it sort of has to take that path. It can’t feel guilt, so if it makes a decision to blackmail somebody, or launch a weapon, it can’t feel the effects of that. It doesn’t have any instincts saying that it just can’t do that or else it will feel this way, and the feeling of guilt is just too rough. 

Another thing we should keep in mind is: would you let your average employee do this thing, or have access to this information, or these tools or whatnot. I think that while the intelligence of AI, and the speed at which it processes is above average, at this point we’ve seen that the ethical aspect of AI is not. We can’t trust it to make these decisions on its own. We should make sure to reflect that trust in what the agent is allowed to do on its own. If you can’t trust a normal employee to do these things on their own, then maybe you shouldn’t trust the AI to do it either. I think that could be a good benchmark test. You would never give a random employee access to everybody’s email, so maybe don’t let AI have it either.

I think one other problem is in how much people just trust AI. I think that in a world with so much science fiction, and with such excellent public-facing results from so many of these companies, people think that AI is better at things than it actually is. I think this is a really dangerous outlook for society to have. We give AI more control over our lives than it ought to have when we think we can just trust it to be okay. Associated with this is the idea that AI is trained on human data. And in this world, especially with everybody’s fascination with true crime and everything like that, we should be aware that what we are feeding into AI is the average of humanity. It may be more intelligent, but it has the average personality, values, and instincts of the human population, and that’s not excellent. We’re going to find it hard to train AI to be the right type of agent when the world doesn’t reflect that.

One of the problems that I think could come up with human in the loop is that AI produces too much too quickly for us to actually verify it all. If it writes code, we just can’t check that it does what it should, or that it doesn’t do something bad like steal personal information, or any of the probably hundreds of other ways that it could crash or do something bad. Even when humans have to check decisions, we can’t check through all the reasoning or issues, we just have to take it at face value and say yes or no. Related to this is one of the ideas that maybe AI shouldn’t be replacing humans, but being treated as an actual tool. Maybe one way to keep AI aligned is to make sure it’s never working alone, but in conjunction with human activity.

One other thing I liked from the discussion was the idea of accountability and responsibility. I think that some of the reason that humans don’t often do bad things is that they understand the consequences, or have some perception of the feeling of responsibility. I think this goes back to the idea of feelings. Humans can feel responsible for certain groups or actions that they need to be aware of, but I don’t think that AI can, not in the same way. I will also say that I don’t think AI agents understand the ideas of consequences. If they do something wrong, it’s unclear how we’re supposed to punish them. Are they accountable for that action? If AI blackmails you, well, blackmail is illegal, but who do the police arrest? I think that maybe holding the companies who design the AI accountable would encourage these companies to adopt good practices. I also think there’s wisdom in holding accountable whoever made the decision “Let’s give AI access to all our emails”. I think this would encourage those people to think more long term about the consequences and potential errors of AI use. I wonder if maybe we could hold the AI accountable. If we could find something that acts as an actual motivator or bad thing that happens to the AI, then maybe it would encourage it to follow its ethical and legal obligations. Maybe if the AI has such a sense of self preservation, we could use that as a punishment. If you do something illegal, then maybe that model gets shut down for a few years, or maybe it gets destroyed completely. Of course, I feel like this would open its own host of problems. What if this actually encourages the AI to just become a better criminal? That’s not good. Can we legally destroy or remove assets from groups like Anthropic or OpenAI? What if those companies find a work around that encourages bad behaviour, like hiding back up servers? I think this conversation needs to be had, though. If we’re going to treat AI like it has human intelligence, then it also needs to be accountable to the laws, or even consequences like being fired from a job for being a bad employee - but even then, do people think they need AI too much to do that? It just makes me wonder if the best solution is to treat AI more like a human or less like one. 

Also, I feel like if we’re going to make any progress on the safe use of AI, we need to know that our government is interested in the safe use of AI. They can’t ignore the AI problem, they need to be passing regulation. But at the same time, I feel like there are so many issues with the government (and with corporations too), that I’m not sure I trust the government to have my best interest in mind. I’m also not sure I think the government is smart enough (or listens to people smart enough) to realize all the flaws and issues with AI right now, and interact with it responsibly. On that note, can we even trust the founders of these AI companies? Their CEO’s, their shareholder interests?

I feel like I’ve discussed a lot of thoughts but not the actual question. What am I willing to let AI do, and what will I not let it do? I’m not sure there’s any sort of decision I would let AI make without supervision. I think I would let it gather information, summarize sources, and present choices, but I wouldn’t actually let it make the decision. Maybe in cases where there is easy enough redress of issues, such as the autograder tool we use in class, where we can get something fixed by just messaging Rebekah. I would let it make decisions that can be understood (not just technically supervised) by some sort of human agent, especially when they are reversible. But I think we need to weigh our options. Yes, some of the bad things won’t happen often, but in some cases, having something bad happen even once may not be worth the risk. With AI, I think we always need to plan for the worst, even when hoping for the best.
